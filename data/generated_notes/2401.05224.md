# Summary

This paper investigates whether unaligned uni-modal [[vision encoders]] and [[language encoders]] can communicate in a [[zero-shot]] manner by leveraging their inherent [[semantic similarity]] in representing the physical world. The authors use [[Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)]] to demonstrate that the [[latent spaces]] of unaligned encoders exhibit comparable semantic similarity to [[aligned encoders]] like [[CLIP]]. They propose two [[training-free]] methods—a [[Fast Quadratic Assignment Problem (QAP) optimization]] and a [[Localized CKA metric-based matching/retrieval]]—to connect these encoders. The main finding is that [[zero-shot communication]] between unaligned [[encoder]] [[latent spaces]] is achievable for various [[cross-modal retrieval]] and classification tasks.

# Main Contribution

- Proposing a matching method that maximizes [[CKA]] by formulating it as a [[Quadratic Assignment Problem (QAP)]] with improved transformations and normalizations.
- Introducing a novel [[local CKA metric]] for retrieval between unaligned [[embedding spaces]], demonstrating superior performance on [[COCO]] caption image retrieval.
- Benchmarking the method on [[COCO]], [[NoCaps]], and [[ImageNet-100]], demonstrating [[zero-shot communication]] between [[encoder]] [[latent spaces]] for various downstream tasks.
- Demonstrating a practical application for [[cross-lingual image retrieval]] using [[Sentence Transformers]] and a [[CLIP]] [[vision encoder]].

# Research Problem

The paper addresses the question of whether an inherent alignment exists between independently trained uni-modal [[vision encoders]] and [[language encoders]], given that they both represent the same physical world. While [[aligned encoders]] like [[CLIP]] achieve [[statistical similarity]] through [[contrastive loss]] training, this property is absent in unaligned models, typically requiring additional training of mapping networks or "stitching layers." The core research problem is to determine if this training step is truly necessary, and if [[unaligned encoders]] can be connected in a [[zero-shot]] manner by exploiting their underlying [[semantic similarity]], thereby bridging a gap in [[representation learning]] for [[cross-modal retrieval]] without explicit alignment training.

# Method

The proposed method leverages [[Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)]] to measure the [[representation similarity]] between unaligned [[vision]] and [[language encoder]] [[latent spaces]]. [[CKA]] is chosen over other metrics like [[Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA)]] due to its robustness in measuring meaningful similarities. The authors first demonstrate that [[CKA]] scores between unaligned encoders can be comparable to those of [[aligned encoders]] like [[CLIP]], indicating an underlying [[semantic similarity]]. They also show that [[CKA]] is sensitive to the ordering of data, with maximum scores achieved on ground-truth pairings, motivating the search for an optimal permutation.

To exploit this sensitivity, the paper frames the problem of connecting unaligned encoders as a [[seeded graph-matching]] task, aiming to find a [[permutation]] that maximizes the [[CKA]] score between the image and text representations. Two main approaches are proposed:
1.  **[[Fast Quadratic Assignment Problem (QAP) optimization]]**: This method provides a global matching solution. The problem of maximizing [[CKA]] is reformulated as a [[quadratic optimization problem]]. The authors introduce specific transformations and normalizations to improve the matching performance of the [[QAP]] algorithm, which seeks the optimal [[permutation]] across a query set.
2.  **[[Localized CKA metric-based matching/retrieval]]**: This approach focuses on aligning images and captions at a more granular level, suitable for scenarios where a single query image is matched against a set of captions or vice versa. It uses a novel [[local CKA metric]] to perform retrieval between two unaligned [[embedding spaces]]. Unlike prior work that relies on [[relative representations]] and [[cosine similarity]] to anchors, this method uses an implicit measure of similarity of similarities, aiming for greater robustness to anchor selection and noise. Crucially, both methods operate in a [[training-free]] manner, distinguishing them from approaches involving [[model stitching]] or training linear mapping layers.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The method was benchmarked on several datasets to evaluate its effectiveness across different tasks. For [[cross-modal retrieval]] and caption matching, the [[COCO]] dataset (specifically the 5k image-caption pairs from the validation set) and the [[NoCaps]] dataset (for cross-domain caption retrieval) were used. For image classification, the [[ImageNet-100]] dataset was employed.

A key experimental setup involved demonstrating the sensitivity of [[CKA]] to data ordering. The authors measured the [[CKA]] score between [[DINOv2]] (a [[vision encoder]]) and [[All-Roberta-large-v1]] (a [[language encoder]]) on 5k [[COCO]] image-caption pairs. They systematically shuffled varying percentages (0% to 100%) of the data to create wrong matches while keeping the rest aligned. This experiment showed a monotonic decrease in [[CKA]] score with increased shuffling, confirming that [[CKA]] can identify correct pairings.

# Key Results

-   [[CKA]] analysis revealed that the [[representation spaces]] of unaligned [[vision]] and [[language encoders]] (e.g., [[DINOv2]] and [[All-Roberta-large-v1]]) exhibit [[semantic similarity]] comparable to that of [[aligned encoders]] like [[CLIP]] on [[COCO]] image-caption pairs.
-   The [[CKA]] score is highly sensitive to data ordering; for [[DINOv2]] and [[All-Roberta-large-v1]] on [[COCO]], the [[CKA]] score dropped from 0.72 (0% shuffling) to 0.01 (100% shuffling), demonstrating its ability to identify correct pairings.
-   The proposed [[Localized CKA metric-based matching/retrieval]] method demonstrated superior performance compared to [[relative representations]] on the [[COCO]] caption image retrieval task.
-   The methods achieved [[zero-shot communication]] between [[encoder]] [[latent spaces]] across various downstream tasks, including [[cross-domain caption retrieval]] on [[NoCaps]] and [[image classification]] on [[ImageNet-100]], despite not being explicitly optimized for alignment.
-   A practical application was successfully demonstrated for [[cross-lingual image retrieval]] by combining [[Sentence Transformers]] trained in various languages with a [[CLIP]] [[vision encoder]] trained only in English.

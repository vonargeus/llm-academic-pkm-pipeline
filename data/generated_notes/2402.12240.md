# Summary

This paper addresses the critical issue of [[Reasoning Shortcuts]] in [[Neuro-Symbolic AI]], where models learn unintended concept semantics that still satisfy symbolic constraints, leading to overconfidence in incorrect internal logic. The authors propose [[bears]] ([[BE Aware of Reasoning Shortcuts]]), an [[ensembling technique]] that encourages models to be uncertain about concepts affected by these shortcuts. Their main finding is that [[bears]] significantly improves [[uncertainty calibration]] and enables efficient [[active learning]] for concept supervision without sacrificing [[prediction accuracy]].

# Main Contribution

- Shifted the research focus from purely mitigating [[Reasoning Shortcuts]] to making models aware of them through [[uncertainty calibration]].
- Developed [[bears]], a diversified [[ensembling]] method that identifies [[semantic ambiguity]] in [[neural predicates]] without requiring dense supervision.
- Demonstrated that RS-awareness facilitates targeted [[active learning]], allowing models to identify which concept annotations are most needed for mitigation.

# Research Problem

[[Neuro-Symbolic AI]] models often exploit [[Reasoning Shortcuts]] because the provided [[symbolic knowledge]] and training data are insufficient to uniquely pin down the intended semantics of latent concepts. This leads to models that achieve high label accuracy but rely on "wrong" reasons (e.g., confusing a pedestrian for a red light because both require a "stop" action), resulting in overconfidence and poor generalization. This paper addresses the gap in current literature where existing mitigation strategies either require costly dense supervision or fail to signal when a model's internal reasoning is untrustworthy.

# Method

The authors introduce [[bears]] ([[BE Aware of Reasoning Shortcuts]]), which replaces the standard [[concept extractor]] with a [[diversified ensemble]]. The core idea is to encourage different members of the ensemble to explore the space of concept mappings that are consistent with the [[symbolic knowledge]] $K$. 

Unlike standard [[ensembling]], [[bears]] is designed to ensure that [[concept-level confidence]] is low when multiple conflicting concept assignments could explain the observed labels. This is achieved by training the ensemble to be "aware" of [[semantic ambiguity]]. When the ensemble members disagree on a concept but agree on the final label, it indicates a [[Reasoning Shortcuts]] is present. This allows the model to maintain high [[prediction accuracy]] while providing a calibrated uncertainty signal that warns the user when the internal [[neural predicates]] are unreliable.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The primary evaluation is conducted on the [[BDD-OIA]] dataset, a high-stakes [[autonomous driving]] task where a model must predict safe actions (e.g., stop, move forward) based on dashcam images. The [[symbolic knowledge]] encodes safety constraints, such as "if a pedestrian or a red light is detected, the vehicle must stop." The setup specifically tests the model's ability to distinguish between these concepts when they both lead to the same supervised label. The authors also evaluate the method across four different [[Neuro-Symbolic AI]] datasets to ensure generalizability.

# Evaluation Metrics

- [[prediction accuracy]]: Measures the performance on the downstream task (e.g., driving actions).
- [[concept-level confidence]]: Evaluates how certain the model is about its internal concept predictions.
- [[uncertainty calibration]]: Assesses the alignment between the model's confidence and the actual ground-truth correctness of the concepts, specifically looking for lower confidence in the presence of [[Reasoning Shortcuts]].

# Key Results

- [[bears]] successfully reduces overconfidence in models affected by [[Reasoning Shortcuts]], allowing users to identify and distrust low-quality concept predictions.
- The method improves [[uncertainty calibration]] across several state-of-the-art [[Neuro-Symbolic AI]] architectures.
- In [[active learning]] experiments, [[bears]] enabled the model to select the most informative samples for concept annotation, significantly reducing the amount of human labeling required to fix the shortcuts.
- Quantitative results show that [[bears]] maintains high [[prediction accuracy]] while providing a much more reliable safety signal than standard neural or NeSy baselines.

# Limitations

The method focuses on making models aware of [[Reasoning Shortcuts]] to enable identification and distrust of low-quality concepts rather than attempting to avoid or eliminate the shortcuts entirely. It also assumes that the ensemble is diverse enough to capture the relevant [[semantic ambiguity]] inherent in the task.

# Summary

[[Neuro-Symbolic (NeSy) predictors]] often exhibit [[overconfidence]] in [[concepts]] affected by [[Reasoning Shortcuts (RSs)]], which compromises their [[reliability]] and [[generalization]]. This paper introduces [[bears (BE Aware of Reasoning Shortcuts)]], an [[ensembling technique]] designed to make [[NeSy models]] aware of these [[RSs]] by calibrating their [[concept-level confidence]] without requiring costly [[dense supervision]]. [[bears]] encourages models to be uncertain about [[concepts]] impacted by [[semantic ambiguity]], thereby improving [[RS-awareness]] and facilitating more efficient [[active learning]] for [[concept supervision]].

# Main Contribution

- Shift focus from [[RS mitigation]] to [[RS awareness]] and demonstrate that state-of-the-art [[NeSy predictors]] are not [[RS-aware]].
- Propose [[bears]], a method that improves [[RS-awareness]] of [[NeSy predictors]] without relying on [[dense supervision]].
- Show that [[bears]] outperforms state-of-the-art [[uncertainty calibration]] methods across various tasks and architectures.
- Enable intelligent acquisition of [[concept annotations]], thereby lowering the cost of supervised [[RS mitigation]].

# Research Problem

The paper addresses the critical problem that [[Neuro-Symbolic (NeSy) models]] are often [[overconfident]] about [[concepts]] that are affected by [[Reasoning Shortcuts (RSs)]]. These [[RSs]] occur when models learn [[concepts]] with unintended semantics, even while achieving high [[prediction accuracy]] on the main task, leading to compromised [[reliability]] and poor [[generalization]], especially in out-of-distribution scenarios. Existing strategies to mitigate [[RSs]] are either costly, requiring extensive [[dense supervision]], or unreliable, offering no guarantees. This gap highlights the need for a method that can make [[NeSy models]] aware of their [[RSs]] without incurring prohibitive annotation costs.

# Method

The proposed method, [[bears (BE Aware of Reasoning Shortcuts)]], is an [[ensembling technique]] designed to improve the [[RS-awareness]] of [[Neuro-Symbolic (NeSy) predictors]]. Instead of attempting to avoid [[RSs]] altogether, [[bears]] focuses on making models assign lower [[concept-level confidence]] to [[concepts]] affected by [[semantic ambiguity]] or [[RSs]], while maintaining high [[prediction accuracy]].

[[bears]] achieves this by replacing the standard [[concept extraction module]] within a [[NeSy predictor]] with a diversified [[ensemble]]. This [[ensemble]] is specifically trained to encourage the [[uncertainty]] of the [[concepts]] to be proportional to how strongly they are impacted by [[RSs]]. The core idea is that by having multiple diverse [[concept extractors]] in the [[ensemble]], the model can better identify when different interpretations of a [[concept]] are consistent with the [[symbolic knowledge]] and observed labels, thus signaling [[semantic ambiguity]] and potential [[RSs]] through increased [[uncertainty]]. This approach allows [[bears]] to calibrate the model's [[concept-level confidence]] without requiring explicit [[concept supervision]] during training, a key differentiator from prior costly mitigation strategies.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The effectiveness of [[bears]] was evaluated on four distinct [[NeSy data sets]], including a high-stakes autonomous driving task called [[BDD-OIA]]. Experiments involved applying [[bears]] to three different state-of-the-art [[NeSy architectures]] to demonstrate its general applicability. The setup aimed to show that [[bears]] could successfully improve the [[RS-awareness]] of these diverse models across various tasks.

# Evaluation Metrics

- **[[Concept-level confidence]]**: Used to assess how certain the model is about its [[concept]] predictions, with lower confidence desired for [[concepts]] affected by [[RSs]].
- **[[RS-awareness]]**: A primary metric to quantify how well the model recognizes and expresses [[uncertainty]] about [[concepts]] that are prone to [[Reasoning Shortcuts]].
- **[[Prediction accuracy]]**: Measured to ensure that improving [[RS-awareness]] does not compromise the model's overall performance on the main prediction task.

# Key Results

- State-of-the-art [[NeSy predictors]] were empirically shown to lack [[RS-awareness]], predicting [[concepts]] affected by [[RSs]] with high [[confidence]].
- [[bears]] successfully improved the [[RS-awareness]] of three state-of-the-art [[NeSy architectures]] across four [[NeSy data sets]], including the [[BDD-OIA]] autonomous driving task.
- [[bears]] demonstrated superior performance compared to existing state-of-the-art [[uncertainty calibration]] methods in improving [[RS-awareness]].
- The method enables a simple but effective [[active learning]] strategy for acquiring [[concept annotations]], thereby reducing the cost associated with supervised [[RS mitigation]].

# Limitations

While [[bears]] significantly improves [[RS-awareness]], it does not aim to *avoid* [[Reasoning Shortcuts]] altogether, but rather to make the model *aware* of them. The ultimate goal of full [[RS mitigation]] still often requires [[concept supervision]], though [[bears]] facilitates its intelligent acquisition. Furthermore, the effectiveness of [[bears]] relies on the ability of the diversified [[ensemble]] to capture and express [[semantic ambiguity]], which might be challenging in highly complex or subtle [[RS]] scenarios.

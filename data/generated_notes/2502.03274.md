# Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of formally verifying the [[Robustness|robustness]] of [[Neuro-Symbolic Artificial Intelligence|probabilistic Neuro-Symbolic (NeSy)]] systems against [[Input Perturbations|input perturbations]]. The authors prove that the decision version of this verification task is [[NPPP-completeness|NPPP-complete]], making exact solutions computationally expensive. To overcome this, they propose a scalable, [[Relaxation-based verification|relaxation-based]] approach that compiles the entire NeSy system—including the neural perception and symbolic reasoning layers—into a single [[Computational graph|computational graph]]. Their main finding is that this method scales exponentially better than exact solver-based solutions, enabling the verification of safety properties in high-dimensional domains like [[Autonomous driving dataset|autonomous driving]].

# Main Contribution

- Complexity analysis proving that the decision version of probabilistic NeSy verification is [[NPPP-completeness|NPPP-complete]].
- Development of the first approximate, [[Relaxation-based verification|relaxation-based verification]] framework specifically designed for probabilistic NeSy systems.
- A novel method to compile symbolic knowledge into [[Arithmetic Circuits|Arithmetic Circuits]] that integrate directly with neural networks into a unified [[Computational graph|computational graph]].
- Empirical evidence demonstrating that the proposed method achieves exponential improvements in [[Scalability|scalability]] over traditional [[Satisfiability Modulo Theories|SMT]] or [[Mixed Integer Linear Programming|MILP]] solver-based solutions.

# Research Problem

The paper tackles the problem of ensuring [[AI Safety|AI safety]] in systems that combine neural perception with symbolic reasoning. While [[Neural Network Verification|NN verification]] techniques exist for purely sub-symbolic models, they do not account for the probabilistic symbolic layers that process [[Latent concepts|latent concepts]]. Verifying that a small change in input (e.g., image noise) does not change the logical conclusion of a NeSy system is difficult because the underlying [[Weighted Model Counting|Weighted Model Counting (WMC)]] is computationally hard. This gap prevents the deployment of NeSy systems in mission-critical applications where formal [[Robustness|robustness]] guarantees are required.

# Method

The authors propose a pipeline that leverages [[Knowledge Compilation|Knowledge Compilation (KC)]] to bridge the gap between neural outputs and symbolic reasoning. First, the symbolic knowledge base is compiled into a tractable representation, specifically an [[Arithmetic Circuits|Arithmetic Circuit (AC)]]. In this circuit, logical AND/OR operations are replaced by multiplication and addition, and negations are handled via subtraction ($1-x$). 

This [[Arithmetic Circuits|AC]] is then integrated with the neural network to form a single, end-to-end [[Computational graph|computational graph]]. Because the AC consists only of standard arithmetic operations, it is amenable to [[Relaxation-based verification|relaxation-based techniques]] such as [[Interval Bound Propagation|Interval Bound Propagation (IBP)]]. This allows the system to propagate bounds from the input perturbations through the neural layers and the symbolic reasoning layer simultaneously. By using off-the-shelf [[Neural Network Verification|NN verifiers]] on this unified graph, the method computes over-approximated bounds on the output probabilities, providing a sound (though incomplete) guarantee of robustness.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The method was evaluated using two distinct setups:
1.  **[[Standard NeSy benchmark domain]]**: Used to compare the [[Scalability|scalability]] of the relaxation-based approach against exact solvers. This typically involves tasks where neural networks identify digits or objects that are then summed or related via logical rules.
2.  **[[Autonomous driving dataset]]**: A real-world application involving high-dimensional dashcam images. The system includes an [[Object detection network]] and an [[Action selector network]]. The goal was to verify safety properties, such as ensuring the "brake" action probability remains high when a "red light" and "car in front" are detected, even under input noise.

# Evaluation Metrics

- **[[Scalability]]**: How the [[Verification time]] scales with the size of the symbolic formula and the number of latent concepts.
- **[[Verification time]]**: The total time required to compute a robustness certificate.
- **[[Robustness guarantees]]**: The ability of the system to prove that the probability of a specific symbolic output remains within a safe threshold under $\epsilon$-perturbations.

# Key Results

- **Complexity**: Formally established the [[NPPP-completeness|NPPP-complete]] nature of the probabilistic NeSy verification problem.
- **Performance**: The proposed method demonstrated exponential improvements in [[Scalability|scalability]]. In benchmarks where exact solvers timed out as the number of symbolic variables increased, the relaxation-based method provided results in near-constant or linear time.
- **Real-world Application**: Successfully verified safety properties on the [[Autonomous driving dataset]], handling high-dimensional inputs and complex network architectures that are beyond the reach of current exact NeSy verifiers.
- **Soundness**: Confirmed that the [[Relaxation-based techniques|relaxation-based]] bounds, while not exact, are sufficient to provide formal safety certificates in practical scenarios.

# Limitations

The primary limitation is that the method is an approximate, [[Relaxation-based verification|relaxation-based]] approach rather than an exact solver. This means it is "incomplete"—it may fail to prove a robustness property even if it holds (false negatives). This is a necessary trade-off to manage the [[NPPP-completeness|NPPP-complete]] complexity of the problem, but it may require tighter relaxations or specialized training to be effective in all scenarios.

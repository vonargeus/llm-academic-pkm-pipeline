# Summary

This paper investigates the theoretical reasons why [[linear RNN]]s (LRNNs) are more parallelizable than traditional [[nonlinear RNN]]s by mapping them to standard [[computational complexity theory]] classes. The authors demonstrate that LRNNs correspond to log-depth arithmetic circuits, allowing for efficient parallelization, whereas nonlinear RNNs can solve [[L-complete]] or [[P-complete]] problems, which creates a fundamental barrier to parallelization. The study provides a fine-grained expressivity hierarchy for different LRNN variants and validates these findings through synthetic benchmarks.

# Main Contribution

- Established a formal connection between different [[RNN]] architectures and standard [[computational complexity theory]] classes.
- Demonstrated that [[linear RNN]]s are equivalent to log-depth arithmetic circuits, explaining their high [[parallelizability]].
- Identified that [[nonlinear RNN]]s solve [[L-complete]] or [[P-complete]] problems, creating a theoretical barrier to efficient parallelization.
- Provided a fine-grained expressivity hierarchy, showing [[diagonal-plus-low-rank (DPLR) LRNNs]] are [[PNC1-complete]] while [[permutation-diagonal (PD) LRNNs]] are [[NC1-complete]].

# Research Problem

The research addresses the fundamental trade-off between the expressive power of [[RNN]]s and their ability to be parallelized during training. While [[linear RNN]]s have gained popularity for their performance and parallelizability, it remains unclear why they possess these advantages over traditional [[nonlinear RNN]]s. This paper fills the gap by using [[circuit complexity]] to characterize the computational limits of these architectures, providing a theoretical foundation for future [[LLM architecture]] design.

# Method

The authors model [[RNN]]s using [[arithmetic closed form]] functions over a semiring, allowing them to analyze the depth of the circuits required to compute the hidden states. They categorize architectures based on their parameterization: [[linear RNN]]s (LRNNs) are defined by linear state updates, while [[nonlinear RNN]]s utilize non-linear functions like [[ReLU]].

The analysis distinguishes between [[diagonal-plus-low-rank (DPLR) LRNNs]] and [[permutation-diagonal (PD) LRNNs]]. By mapping these to complexity classes like [[NC1]], [[PNC1]], [[L]], and [[P]], the authors determine the minimum circuit depth required for simulation. They also associate each class with an [[automata theory]] model, such as [[weighted finite automaton]] (WFA) or [[deterministic WFA]] (DWFA), to provide a lower bound on their capabilities.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The authors evaluate their theoretical findings using two synthetic tasks:
- **[[Graph connectivity task]]**: An [[L-complete]] problem used to test the limits of [[nonlinear RNN]]s.
- **[[Iterated matrix multiplication problem]]**: A [[PNC1-complete]] problem used to differentiate the expressivity of various [[LRNN]] variants.

# Evaluation Metrics

The paper primarily uses theoretical complexity bounds (circuit depth and class completeness) as the main evaluation metric. Empirical performance on synthetic tasks is used to confirm that the observed model behavior aligns with the predicted complexity-theoretic limits.

# Key Results

- [[linear RNN]]s are in [[PNC1]], incurring only a negligible $O(\log^* n)$ depth overhead compared to transformers.
- [[nonlinear RNN]]s with log precision require $\Omega(\log^2 n)$ depth, while those with polynomial precision require more than polylog depth, assuming standard complexity assumptions.
- [[diagonal-plus-low-rank (DPLR) LRNNs]] are [[PNC1-complete]], whereas [[permutation-diagonal (PD) LRNNs]] are [[NC1-complete]].
- Empirical results confirm that only [[nonlinear RNN]]s solve the [[L-complete]] [[graph connectivity task]], while both [[nonlinear RNN]]s and [[DPLR LRNNs]] solve the [[iterated matrix multiplication problem]].

# Limitations

The theoretical results rely on unproven complexity-theoretic assumptions, specifically that [[PNC1]] $\neq$ [[P]] and [[NC]] $\neq$ [[P]]. Additionally, the analysis assumes specific precision regimes (log vs. polynomial), which may not perfectly reflect the behavior of floating-point arithmetic in practical hardware.

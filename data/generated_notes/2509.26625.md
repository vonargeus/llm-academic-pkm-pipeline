# Summary

This paper investigates the surprising emergence of [[visual priors]] in [[Large Language Models]] (LLMs) trained solely on text. The authors demonstrate that these priors are not monolithic but consist of separable "perception" and "reasoning" components with distinct origins. By conducting over 100 controlled experiments, they find that [[latent visual reasoning]] is primarily driven by reasoning-centric data (e.g., code, math), while perception emerges from broad, diverse corpora. The study culminates in a data-centric pre-training recipe that improves the construction of [[Multimodal Large Language Models]] (MLLMs).

# Main Contribution

- Systematic decomposition of [[visual priors]] into distinct perceptual and reasoning components.
- Identification that [[reasoning-centric data]] is the primary driver for [[latent visual reasoning]] capabilities.
- Introduction of the [[Multi-Level Existence Bench (MLE-Bench)]] for fine-grained evaluation of perceptual abilities.
- Development of a data-centric pre-training recipe to optimize the creation of vision-aware LLMs.

# Research Problem

The research addresses the paradox of how LLMs, despite being trained exclusively on text, acquire latent capabilities to perform visual tasks. While this phenomenon is well-documented, the underlying mechanisms—specifically the origins and structure of these [[visual priors]]—remain poorly understood. This paper fills the gap by deconstructing these priors to determine how specific data distributions and model architectures contribute to the emergence of cross-modal reasoning, providing empirical support for the [[Platonic Representation Hypothesis]].

# Method

The authors employ a rigorous, controlled ablation framework to isolate the factors influencing [[visual priors]]. They train a suite of decoder-only Transformer models (based on the Llama-3 architecture) across five scales (340M to 13B parameters) using a diverse set of 16 data sources. By systematically varying data mixtures, model scales, and training durations, they isolate the impact of specific data categories on visual performance.

To evaluate these models, they introduce the [[Multi-Level Existence Bench (MLE-Bench)]], which probes the model's perceptual depth. They also utilize "blind visual instruction tuning"—a technique where models are fine-tuned on visual tasks without visual input—to probe how LLMs "hack" visual tasks using their internal language-derived reasoning frameworks. This allows for a clear distinction between the model's inherent [[perception prior]] and its ability to perform [[cross-modal alignment]].

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The experiments are conducted using a controlled pre-training environment with 16 distinct data sources, including academic, code, mathematics, and web-crawl data. The default setup uses a 3B parameter model trained on 30B tokens. The study spans 500,000 GPU-hours, covering the full pipeline from initial LLM pre-training to [[visual instruction tuning]]. The [[Multi-Level Existence Bench (MLE-Bench)]] is used as the primary evaluation tool to measure the model's fine-grained perceptual capabilities.

# Evaluation Metrics

- **Perceptual Accuracy:** Measured via the [[Multi-Level Existence Bench (MLE-Bench)]] to assess the model's ability to identify and reason about visual objects.
- **Scaling Trends:** Quantitative analysis of how performance improves relative to model size and data volume.
- **Transferability:** Assessment of how well reasoning-centric pre-training generalizes to downstream visual tasks.

# Key Results

- [[latent visual reasoning]] scales progressively with the volume of [[reasoning-centric data]] (e.g., code, math).
- The [[perception prior]] emerges more diffusely from broad, diverse corpora and is highly sensitive to the choice of [[vision encoder]].
- Textual descriptions of the visual world provide significant performance gains, but these gains saturate rapidly.
- The findings provide strong empirical support for the [[Platonic Representation Hypothesis]], suggesting that LLMs learn a unified internal world model from text that is transferable to vision.

# Limitations

The study focuses primarily on decoder-only Transformer architectures, which may limit the generalizability of the findings to other model families. Additionally, while the authors identify the origins of visual priors, the exact mechanism of how textual statistics map to spatial visual representations remains an area for further investigation.

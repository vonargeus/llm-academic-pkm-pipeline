# Summary

This paper establishes a unified formal framework for comparing and analyzing [[Differentiable Logics]] and [[Fuzzy Logics]] using [[Dependent Type Theory]]. By leveraging the [[Rocq proof assistant]] and the [[MathComp library]], the authors provide a rigorous foundation that bridges the gap between algebraic, proof-theoretic, and analytic properties of these logics. The work introduces new [[Sequent calculi]] and [[Hypersequent calculi]] for machine-learning-oriented logics like [[DL2]] and [[STL]], while formalizing key analytical tools such as [[L’Hôpital’s rule]].

# Main Contribution

- Unified formalization of [[Fuzzy Logics]] and [[Differentiable Logics]] using [[Dependent Type Theory]].
- Algebraic characterization of [[Differentiable Logics]] via [[Residuated lattices]].
- Development of new sound [[Sequent calculi]] for [[DL2]] and [[STL]].
- Formalization of [[L’Hôpital’s rule]] within the [[MathComp library]].

# Research Problem

The research addresses the lack of a unified mathematical framework for [[Differentiable Logics]], which are increasingly used in [[Machine Learning]] for [[Property-guided training]]. While [[Fuzzy Logics]] have a long history of algebraic and proof-theoretic study, modern differentiable logics like [[DL2]] and [[STL]] lack such foundations, leading to discrepancies in how logical properties are translated into [[Loss functions]]. This paper bridges the gap between the algebraic traditions of substructural logics and the analytic requirements of machine learning.

# Method

The authors utilize the [[Rocq proof assistant]] to create a unified language specification for multiple logics, including [[Yager logic]], [[Lukasiewicz logic]], [[Gödel logic]], [[Product logic]], [[DL2]], and [[STL]]. They employ [[Intrinsic typing]] to ensure that only valid, well-typed formulas are constructed within the framework.

Algebraically, the approach maps [[Differentiable Logics]] to [[Residuated lattices]], a structure traditionally used for [[Fuzzy Logics]]. This allows for a comparative inventory of structural properties across different logical systems. Analytically, the authors formalize properties involving limits and differentiation, including the formalization of [[L’Hôpital’s rule]] to support the analysis of logical connectives.

Proof-theoretically, the authors extend existing methods by proposing new [[Sequent calculi]] and [[Hypersequent calculi]] specifically for [[DL2]] and [[STL]]. They prove the soundness of these calculi within the [[Rocq]] framework, providing a formal basis for the translation of logical properties into optimization tasks.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The paper is a theoretical formalization study and does not utilize empirical datasets. The "experimental" setup consists of the formal verification environment provided by the [[Rocq proof assistant]] and the [[MathComp library]], where the logical systems are encoded and their properties are proven.

# Evaluation Metrics

- **Soundness**: The primary metric is the formal proof of soundness for the proposed [[Sequent calculi]] and [[Hypersequent calculi]] within the [[Rocq]] environment.
- **Algebraic Mapping**: The ability to successfully map [[Differentiable Logics]] to [[Residuated lattices]] serves as a qualitative metric for the unification of the framework.

# Key Results

- Successfully unified the formalization of diverse logics ([[Yager]], [[Lukasiewicz]], [[Gödel]], [[Product]], [[DL2]], [[STL]]) under a single [[Dependent type theory]] framework.
- Established that [[Differentiable Logics]] can be characterized by [[Residuated lattices]], providing semantic uniformity.
- Proved the soundness of new [[Sequent calculi]] for [[DL2]] and [[STL]].
- Demonstrated that the n-ary conjunction of [[STL]] converges to a minimum as the parameter tends to infinity.

# Limitations

The paper focuses primarily on the theoretical formalization and proof-theoretic foundations of these logics. It does not provide an empirical performance evaluation of these logics in practical, large-scale [[Machine Learning]] tasks, leaving the bridge between formal verification and real-world training efficiency as an open area for future work.

# Summary

This paper introduces [[Flow Policy Optimization (FPO)]], an on-policy [[Reinforcement Learning]] algorithm that integrates [[Flow Matching]] into the [[policy gradient]] framework. By replacing computationally expensive exact likelihood calculations with a surrogate ratio derived from the [[Conditional Flow Matching (CFM)]] loss, the method enables the training of expressive, [[stochastic policy optimization]]-based models. The authors demonstrate that FPO allows for the learning of [[multimodal action distribution]]s and performs robustly in high-dimensional [[continuous control]] tasks compared to standard [[Gaussian policies]].

# Main Contribution

- Introduction of [[Flow Policy Optimization (FPO)]] for training flow-based policies within the [[PPO-clip]] framework.
- Integration of [[Conditional Flow Matching (CFM)]] as a surrogate for log-likelihood, bypassing the need for exact likelihood computation.
- A sampling-agnostic design that allows for flexible [[ODE integration]] during both training and inference.
- Empirical validation showing superior performance in [[multimodal action distribution]] modeling and under-conditioned [[continuous control]] tasks.

# Research Problem

The research addresses the difficulty of applying modern [[Generative Modeling]] techniques, specifically [[Diffusion Models]] and [[Flow Matching]], to [[Reinforcement Learning]] policies. Traditional [[policy gradient]] methods rely on exact likelihoods, which are computationally prohibitive for complex flow-based models. This creates a gap where practitioners are often forced to use simple [[Gaussian policies]] that lack the expressivity required for complex, [[multimodal action distribution]]-heavy environments.

# Method

[[Flow Policy Optimization (FPO)]] reframes policy optimization by treating the [[vector field]] of a flow model as the policy. During rollouts, the model transforms Gaussian noise into actions via a sequence of learned transformations. Unlike prior work that treats the denoising process as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), FPO treats the sampling procedure as a black box, allowing it to remain agnostic to the specific [[ODE integration]] or sampling method used.

The core of the method is the replacement of the standard likelihood ratio in [[PPO-clip]] with a proxy ratio, $\hat{r}_{FPO}$, derived from the [[Conditional Flow Matching (CFM)]] loss. This loss, $\hat{L}_{CFM, \theta}$, acts as a surrogate for the log-likelihood, effectively steering the probability flow toward high-reward actions by minimizing the distance between the model's predicted velocity and the target conditional flow.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The authors evaluate FPO across a range of environments to test its scalability and expressivity:
- **[[GridWorld]]**: Used to probe the policy's ability to learn [[multimodal action distribution]]s in states with multiple optimal actions.
- **[[MuJoCo Playground]]**: A suite of 10 [[continuous control]] tasks, including high-dimensional humanoid locomotion, used to validate performance against standard baselines.
- The experiments focus on training from scratch, comparing the performance of flow-based policies against traditional [[Gaussian policies]].

# Evaluation Metrics

- **Cumulative Reward**: Used to measure the overall performance and convergence speed of the policy in [[continuous control]] tasks.
- **Action Distribution Analysis**: Qualitative and quantitative assessment of the policy's ability to capture multimodality in [[GridWorld]].
- **Robustness/Stability**: Evaluated through the success of training in high-dimensional domains like humanoid control.

# Key Results

- FPO successfully trains diffusion-style policies from scratch in high-dimensional [[continuous control]] tasks.
- In [[GridWorld]], FPO learns [[multimodal action distribution]]s, whereas [[Gaussian policies]] fail to capture multiple optimal modes.
- In humanoid control, FPO enables single-stage training of under-conditioned policies where only root-level commands are provided, outperforming standard [[Gaussian policies]].
- The method demonstrates stable training dynamics similar to [[PPO-clip]], serving as a drop-in replacement for existing policy architectures.

# Limitations

The authors note that while FPO is robust, there are trade-offs in using flow-based models compared to simpler [[Gaussian policies]], particularly regarding computational overhead during inference. Additionally, the paper discusses potential challenges in scaling to extremely high-dimensional control spaces where the complexity of the learned [[vector field]] may impact training stability.

# Summary

The paper introduces [[Unified Latents (UL)]], a framework for learning latent representations that are jointly regularized by a [[diffusion prior]] and decoded by a [[diffusion decoder]]. By linking the encoder's output noise to the prior's minimum noise level, the authors derive a training objective that provides a tight upper bound on the latent [[bitrate]]. This approach allows for a systematic navigation of the trade-off between latent information content and reconstruction quality, achieving state-of-the-art results in video and image generation.

# Main Contribution

- Introduced a framework for jointly regularizing [[latent representations]] with a [[diffusion prior]] and [[diffusion decoder]].
- Derived a simple training objective that provides a tight upper bound on latent [[bitrate]].
- Achieved state-of-the-art [[FVD]] on [[Kinetics-600]] and competitive [[FID]] on [[ImageNet-512]].

# Research Problem

The paper addresses the challenge of systematically navigating the trade-off between latent information content and reconstruction quality in [[diffusion models]]. While latent representations are essential for scaling generative models, existing methods like [[Latent Diffusion Model]] often rely on manual KL penalty tuning, making it difficult to reason about the information density of the latents. This paper fills the gap by providing a principled, likelihood-based framework to control this trade-off.

# Method

The [[Unified Latents (UL)]] framework consists of an encoder, a [[diffusion prior]], and a [[diffusion decoder]]. The encoder produces a deterministic latent $z_{clean}$, which is then forward-noised to a specific level, linking the encoder noise to the prior's precision. This allows the [[Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)]] to be reduced to a weighted MSE over noise levels.

The [[diffusion decoder]] models the reconstruction term $p_\theta(x|z_0)$ by conditioning on both the noisy data $x_t$ and the latent $z_0$. To prevent posterior collapse and manage the information-reconstruction trade-off, the authors employ a [[re-weighted ELBO]] with a loss factor ($c_{lf}$) and sigmoid bias ($b$), which effectively controls how much information is deferred to the latent space versus the decoder.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The authors evaluate their method on [[ImageNet-512]] for image generation and [[Kinetics-600]] for video generation. The setup involves a two-stage training process: first, training the encoder, prior, and decoder jointly; second, training a base model on the resulting latents. The experiments compare the efficiency and quality of [[Unified Latents (UL)]] against standard latent diffusion approaches.

# Evaluation Metrics

- [[FID]] (Fréchet Inception Distance): Used to measure the quality and diversity of generated images.
- [[PSNR]] (Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio): Used to assess the reconstruction quality of the latent-to-image pipeline.
- [[FVD]] (Fréchet Video Distance): Used to evaluate the temporal consistency and quality of generated video sequences.

# Key Results

- Achieved a competitive [[FID]] of 1.4 on [[ImageNet-512]] with high reconstruction quality.
- Set a new state-of-the-art [[FVD]] of 1.3 on [[Kinetics-600]].
- Demonstrated that the model requires fewer training FLOPs compared to models trained on standard [[Stable Diffusion]] latents.

# Limitations

The authors note that while the prior can be used for generation, training a separate base model in a second stage is often preferred for better performance. Additionally, the reliance on specific hyper-parameters ($c_{lf}$ and $b$) to balance the latent information density suggests that the optimal configuration may be sensitive to the specific dataset or task.

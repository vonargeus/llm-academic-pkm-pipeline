# Summary

The paper introduces [[ReDiSC]], a novel [[discrete-domain diffusion]] model designed to improve [[node classification]] by capturing complex [[label dependencies]] in graphs. Unlike existing diffusion models that operate in continuous spaces and suffer from high computational costs, [[ReDiSC]] uses a [[reparameterized masked diffusion model (RMDM)]] to align with the discrete nature of classification tasks. By employing a [[variational expectation-maximization (EM) framework]], the model achieves superior scalability and performance on both [[homophilic graphs]] and [[heterophilic graphs]].

# Main Contribution

- Proposed [[ReDiSC]], the first [[discrete-domain diffusion]] model specifically designed for structured [[node classification]].
- Established a theoretical link between the model's M-step and popular [[GNN]]+[[Label Propagation]] hybrid approaches, providing interpretability.
- Demonstrated superior scalability and performance across various graph types, effectively handling large-scale datasets where previous diffusion methods fail.

# Research Problem

Existing [[diffusion models]] for [[node classification]] often struggle with high computational overhead due to [[manifold-constrained sampling]] and a "semantic mismatch" caused by operating in continuous latent spaces. Furthermore, most standard [[Graph Neural Networks]] assume conditional independence among node labels, failing to account for the inherent correlations between labels in a graph. This paper addresses the gap in developing a scalable, interpretable, and discrete-domain approach that can effectively model these [[label dependencies]] in a [[transductive learning]] setting.

# Method

[[ReDiSC]] models the joint distribution of node labels using a [[reparameterized masked diffusion model (RMDM)]]. This approach replaces continuous-domain operations with a discrete diffusion process, which is more naturally aligned with classification labels. The model is trained using a [[variational expectation-maximization (EM) framework]], where a priority queue is utilized to select informative samples from the variational distributions, enhancing training efficiency.

To address scalability, the [[RMDM]] implements an efficient "labeled-first" inference procedure, which avoids the heavy computational burden of traditional [[manifold-constrained sampling]]. Additionally, the authors introduce a [[time-aware GNN layer]] within the denoiser to handle label denoising across different noise levels. This design provides an explicit connection to [[GNN]]+[[Label Propagation]] hybrids, offering greater transparency into how the model propagates both features and labels.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The paper evaluates [[ReDiSC]] on a variety of benchmarks, including both [[homophilic graphs]] and [[heterophilic graphs]] of varying sizes. The experiments focus on the [[transductive learning]] setting, where both labeled and unlabeled nodes are available during training. The setup compares the proposed method against state-of-the-art [[GNN]] baselines, [[Label Propagation]] methods, and existing diffusion-based models.

# Key Results

- [[ReDiSC]] consistently outperforms state-of-the-art [[GNN]], [[Label Propagation]], and diffusion-based baselines.
- The model demonstrates significant scalability, successfully processing large-scale datasets that are computationally prohibitive for previous structured diffusion methods.
- Theoretical analysis confirms that [[ReDiSC]]'s reverse process acts as an ensemble of [[GNN]]+[[Label Propagation]] hybrids, validating its interpretability.

# Limitations

Existing diffusion-based methods, which [[ReDiSC]] aims to improve, are noted for their high computational costs and lack of interpretability. While [[ReDiSC]] addresses these, the reliance on the [[variational expectation-maximization (EM) framework]] and specific noise schedules may introduce complexity in hyperparameter tuning for diverse graph structures.

# Summary

This paper introduces [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)]] models to address the limitations of large pre-trained [[language models]] in accessing and manipulating knowledge for [[knowledge-intensive NLP]] tasks, such as providing provenance and updating world knowledge. They propose combining a pre-trained [[seq2seq model]] (as [[parametric memory]]) with a [[dense vector index]] of Wikipedia (as [[non-parametric memory]]) accessed by a [[neural retriever]]. The main finding is that [[RAG]] models achieve state-of-the-art results on several [[open-domain Question Answering]] tasks, generate more factual and diverse language, and allow for easy knowledge updates by simply replacing the [[non-parametric memory]].

# Main Contribution

-   Introduced [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)]] models, which combine pre-trained [[parametric memory]] (a [[seq2seq model]]) and [[non-parametric memory]] (a [[dense vector index]]) for [[language generation]].
-   Proposed two [[RAG]] formulations: [[RAG-Sequence]], which conditions on the same retrieved passage for the entire sequence, and [[RAG-Token]], which can use different passages per token.
-   Achieved state-of-the-art results on three [[open-domain QA]] tasks ([[Natural Questions]], [[WebQuestions]], [[CuratedTrec]]), outperforming both parametric [[seq2seq model]]s and task-specific retrieve-and-extract architectures.
-   Demonstrated that [[RAG]] models generate more specific, diverse, and factual language for [[language generation]] tasks and can update their knowledge by replacing the [[non-parametric memory]].

# Research Problem

The paper addresses the inherent limitations of large pre-trained [[language models]] in effectively accessing and precisely manipulating factual knowledge for [[knowledge-intensive NLP]] tasks. While these models store vast amounts of knowledge in their parameters, they struggle with providing provenance for their predictions, cannot easily expand or revise their internal knowledge, and are prone to generating "[[hallucinations]]". Existing hybrid models that combine [[parametric memory]] with [[non-parametric memory]] have primarily focused on extractive tasks, leaving a gap in applying this paradigm to general-purpose [[sequence-to-sequence models]] for [[language generation]].

# Method

The core of the approach is [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)]], which integrates a [[neural retriever]] with a [[seq2seq model]] to generate text conditioned on retrieved documents. The model leverages two main components: a retriever `pη(z|x)` that returns top-K distributions over text passages `z` given a query `x`, and a generator `pθ(yi|x, z, y1:i-1)` that generates the target sequence `y` based on the input `x`, retrieved passage `z`, and previous tokens. The retrieved document `z` is treated as a [[latent variable]] and marginalized during training.

Two distinct [[RAG]] models are proposed based on how the [[latent variable]] `z` is marginalized:
1.  [[RAG-Sequence]]: This model uses the same retrieved document `z` to generate the complete output sequence `y`. It marginalizes over the top-K documents to get the sequence probability `p(y|x)`.
2.  [[RAG-Token]]: This model allows for drawing a different [[latent variable]] (document) for each target token. This enables the generator to synthesize information from multiple retrieved documents when producing an answer.

The retrieval component, `pη(z|x)`, is based on the [[Dense Passage Retriever (DPR)]] [[bi-encoder architecture]]. It uses a [[BERTBASE]]-based document encoder `d(z)` to create dense representations of documents and a [[BERTBASE]]-based query encoder `q(x)` for queries. The top-K documents are found using [[Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS)]] on a [[dense vector index]] of Wikipedia. This retriever is initialized with a pre-trained [[DPR]] model. The generator component, `pθ(yi|x, z, y1:i-1)`, uses [[BART-large]], a pre-trained [[seq2seq transformer]] with 400M parameters. The input `x` and retrieved content `z` are simply concatenated and fed into [[BART]]. Both the [[DPR]] query encoder and the [[BART]] generator are jointly fine-tuned end-to-end by minimizing the negative marginal log-likelihood, while the [[DPR]] document encoder and its index are kept fixed to reduce computational cost.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The [[RAG]] models were fine-tuned and evaluated on a wide range of [[knowledge-intensive NLP]] tasks. For [[open-domain Question Answering]], the datasets used include [[Natural Questions]], [[WebQuestions]], [[CuratedTrec]], and [[TriviaQA]]. For [[language generation]] tasks, experiments were conducted on [[MS-MARCO]] and [[Jeopardy question generation]]. Additionally, the models were evaluated on the [[fact verification]] task using the [[FEVER]] dataset. The [[non-parametric memory]] for the retriever was constructed from a [[dense vector index]] of Wikipedia.

# Evaluation Metrics

The paper reports achieving state-of-the-art results on several [[open-domain QA]] tasks and outperforming baselines. For [[language generation]] tasks, [[RAG]] models are found to generate responses that are more factual, specific, and diverse compared to a [[BART]] baseline. For [[fact verification]] on [[FEVER]], results within 4.3% of state-of-the-art pipeline models are achieved. Specific quantitative metrics like Exact Match or F1 for QA, or BLEU/ROUGE for generation, are not explicitly detailed in the provided text, but the improvements are stated qualitatively and comparatively.

# Key Results

-   Achieved state-of-the-art results on three [[open-domain QA]] tasks: [[Natural Questions]], [[WebQuestions]], and [[CuratedTrec]].
-   Strongly outperformed recent approaches using specialized pre-training objectives on [[TriviaQA]].
-   For [[knowledge-intensive generation]] tasks like [[MS-MARCO]] and [[Jeopardy question generation]], [[RAG]] models generated responses that were more factual, specific, and diverse than a state-of-the-art parametric-only [[BART]] baseline.
-   Achieved results on [[FEVER]] [[fact verification]] within 4.3% of state-of-the-art pipeline models that use strong retrieval supervision.
-   Demonstrated the ability to update the models' knowledge by simply replacing the [[non-parametric memory]] (document index).

# Limitations

The authors note that updating the [[document encoder]] during training is computationally costly, requiring periodic updates to the document index. They chose to keep the [[document encoder]] and index fixed during fine-tuning, which might limit the retriever's ability to adapt to new knowledge representations. Additionally, the reliance on a top-K approximation for marginalizing [[latent variable]]s could potentially miss relevant information beyond the top-K retrieved documents.

# Summary

This paper addresses the scalability bottleneck of [[probabilistic neurosymbolic learning]] (PNL) caused by the exponential-time complexity of exact [[weighted model counting]] (WMC). The authors propose [[A-NESI]] (Approximate Neurosymbolic Inference), a framework that uses neural networks to perform approximate inference in polynomial time while preserving the semantics of probabilistic logics. By training prediction and explanation models on data generated from symbolic background knowledge, A-NESI scales to complex tasks like multi-digit addition, Sudoku, and shortest-path finding. Crucially, it provides symbolic explanations and guarantees logical constraint satisfaction at test time.

# Main Contribution

- Proposes [[A-NESI]], a framework that performs approximate inference in polynomial time without altering the semantics of probabilistic logics.
- Introduces a training algorithm that leverages data generated by background knowledge to train prediction and explanation models.
- Enables the generation of symbolic explanations for predictions.
- Guarantees the satisfaction of logical constraints at test time using a novel symbolic pruner.

# Research Problem

[[Probabilistic Neurosymbolic Learning]] (PNL) frameworks, such as [[DeepProbLog]], successfully integrate [[neural perception]] with [[symbolic reasoning]]. However, they rely on exact inference via [[weighted model counting]] (WMC), which has exponential-time complexity. This computational bottleneck severely limits the scalability of PNL to complex, high-dimensional tasks. The paper addresses this gap by developing a scalable, polynomial-time approximate inference method that maintains the rigorous semantics of probabilistic logic and guarantees safety constraints.

# Method

The proposed method, [[A-NESI]] (Approximate Neurosymbolic Inference), replaces exact [[weighted model counting]] with neural network-based approximate inference. The architecture consists of three main components: a neural perception model ($f_\theta$), a prediction model ($q_\phi$), and an optional explanation model ($q_\psi$). The perception model processes raw inputs (e.g., images) to output a belief distribution $P$ over symbolic representations. The prediction model $q_\phi(y|P)$ then approximates the probability of the final output $y$ given the belief $P$.

To train these models without relying on expensive exact inference during training, A-NESI uses a generative approach. It samples belief distributions $P$ from a prior, uses the symbolic background knowledge to compute likely worlds $w$ and outputs $y$, and trains the prediction and explanation models on this generated dataset. This decouples the neural training from the exponential cost of online symbolic reasoning.

At test time, to ensure safety and logical consistency, A-NESI incorporates a symbolic pruner. This component filters out logically impossible predictions, guaranteeing that the final output strictly satisfies the logical constraints defined in the background knowledge. Additionally, the explanation model $q_\psi(w|y, P)$ identifies the most likely "worlds" (symbolic states) that explain the predicted output, providing interpretability.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The method is evaluated on three challenging neurosymbolic tasks:
1. **Multi-digit [[MNISTAdd]]**: Predicting the sum of two multi-digit numbers represented as sequences of handwritten MNIST digits.
2. **[[Sudoku]]**: Classifying and solving Sudoku puzzles from visual grids.
3. **Shortest Path**: Finding the shortest path in a grid where cell traversability is perceived from images.

The experiments scale these tasks significantly beyond prior benchmarks to test the combinatorial limits of the approximate inference method.

# Evaluation Metrics

- **Accuracy**: Used to measure the correctness of the final symbolic predictions (e.g., sum accuracy, Sudoku grid accuracy, shortest path correctness).
- **Execution Time**: Used to evaluate the computational efficiency and scaling behavior as the problem size (e.g., number of digits or grid size) increases.
- **Constraint Satisfaction Rate**: Used to verify if the test-time predictions strictly adhere to the logical constraints of the background knowledge.

# Key Results

- **Multi-digit [[MNISTAdd]]**: A-NESI successfully scales to addition problems with up to 15 digits, whereas exact inference methods like [[DeepProbLog]] fail to scale beyond 4 digits due to exponential complexity.
- **[[Sudoku]]**: A-NESI scales to 9x9 Sudoku grids, a significant improvement over prior PNL methods which were limited to 4x4 grids.
- **Shortest Path**: A-NESI successfully finds shortest paths in grids up to 30x30, compared to the limit of 12x12 in previous exact PNL systems.
- **Performance and Safety**: The method maintains high predictive accuracy comparable to exact inference while guaranteeing 100% satisfaction of logical constraints at test time without a performance penalty.

# Limitations

The performance of A-NESI relies heavily on the quality of the generated training data from the prior distribution, which may not cover all edge cases if the prior is poorly specified. Additionally, while approximate inference is polynomial-time, training the neural approximation models still requires offline generation of symbolic data, which could be bottlenecked by complex background knowledge during the offline phase.

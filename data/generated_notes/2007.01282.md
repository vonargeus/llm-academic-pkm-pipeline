# Summary

This paper investigates how large [[generative models]] for [[Open Domain Question Answering]] (ODQA), which are typically expensive to train and query due to storing all knowledge in their parameters, can benefit from [[passage retrieval]]. The authors propose a simple two-step [[retrieval-then-generate approach]] using a [[sequence-to-sequence model]] with a novel [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] architecture. They demonstrate that integrating retrieved text passages significantly improves performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on [[Natural Questions]] and [[TriviaQA]] benchmarks. A key finding is that performance continues to improve with an increasing number of retrieved passages, indicating the generative model's effectiveness at aggregating diverse evidence.

# Research Problem

This paper addresses the challenge that while large [[generative models]] have shown competitive performance in [[Open Domain Question Answering]] (ODQA) without relying on [[external knowledge]], they are prohibitively expensive to train and query. This high cost stems from the necessity of storing all factual information within the model's billions of parameters. The core research problem is to investigate the extent to which these powerful generative models can benefit from having access to an external source of knowledge, specifically retrieved text passages, to potentially reduce their reliance on internal knowledge and improve overall efficiency and performance. This work aims to bridge the gap between purely generative and purely extractive retrieval-based ODQA systems.

# Method

The proposed approach to [[Open Domain Question Answering]] is a [[two-step retrieval-then-generate approach]]. First, relevant support passages are retrieved from a large corpus, such as Wikipedia. Second, a [[sequence-to-sequence model]] then generates the answer, conditioned on both the question and the retrieved passages.

For the **retrieval** step, two methods are considered: [[BM25]] and [[DPR]]. [[BM25]] uses [[sparse representations]] based on term and inverse document frequencies, with passages represented as bag-of-words. [[DPR]] (Dense Passage Retriever) uses [[dense embeddings]] computed by two [[BERT]] networks for questions and passages, with ranking based on the dot product of these representations. [[Approximate nearest neighbors]] search is performed using the [[FAISS]] library for [[DPR]].

The **generative model** is based on a pretrained [[sequence-to-sequence network]] like [[T5]] or [[BART]]. The key innovation lies in its architecture, termed [[Fusion-in-Decoder]]. In this design, each retrieved passage and its title are concatenated with the question and processed *independently* by the encoder. Special tokens (`question:`, `title:`, `context:`) are added to delineate these segments. Crucially, the decoder then performs attention over the *concatenation* of the resulting representations from all retrieved passages. This allows for efficient scaling to a large number of contexts, as encoder computation time grows linearly with the number of passages (instead of quadratically with joint encoding), while still enabling the decoder to jointly aggregate evidence from multiple passages. This differs from prior work like [[Min et al. (2020)]] and [[Lewis et al. (2020)]] in how multiple passages are processed by the generative model.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The authors evaluate their [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] approach on several standard [[Open Domain Question Answering]] datasets, using the same settings as [[Lee et al. (2019)]]:
-   [[Natural Questions]] (NQ): Contains questions derived from Google search queries. The open-domain version discards answers longer than 5 tokens.
-   [[TriviaQA]]: Comprises questions from trivia and quiz-league websites. The unfiltered version is used for open-domain QA.
-   [[SQuAD v1.1]]: A reading comprehension dataset where answers are spans from provided paragraphs. For open-domain evaluation, the validation set is used as the test set, and 10% of the training set is reserved for validation.

The retrieval corpus consists of Wikipedia dumps from December 20, 2018, for [[Natural Questions]] and [[TriviaQA]], and December 21, 2016, for [[SQuAD]]. The same preprocessing steps as [[Chen et al. (2017)]] and [[Karpukhin et al. (2020)]] are applied. Experiments also investigate the impact of increasing the number of retrieved passages, testing up to 100 passages.

# Evaluation Metrics

-   [[EM]] (Exact Match): This metric measures the percentage of predictions that exactly match one of the ground truth answers. It is a strict metric commonly used in [[Question Answering]].
-   [[F1]]: This metric calculates the harmonic mean of precision and recall between the predicted answer and the ground truth answer. It is more lenient than [[EM]] and is useful for evaluating answers that are semantically correct but not an exact string match.

# Key Results

-   The [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] model achieved state-of-the-art results on both the [[Natural Questions]] and [[TriviaQA]] open benchmarks.
    -   On [[Natural Questions]], [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (base) achieved 48.2 [[EM]], and [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (large) achieved 51.4 [[EM]].
    -   On [[TriviaQA]] (open domain test set), [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (base) achieved 65.0 [[EM]], and [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (large) achieved 67.6 [[EM]].
    -   On [[TriviaQA]] (hidden test set), [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (base) achieved 77.1 [[EM]] and 53.4 [[F1]], while [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (large) achieved 80.1 [[EM]] and 56.7 [[F1]].
-   Performance of the method significantly improves as the number of retrieved passages increases, up to 100 passages, across all evaluated datasets ([[Natural Questions]], [[TriviaQA]], [[SQuAD]]). This demonstrates the generative model's strong capability for [[evidence aggregation]] from multiple sources.
-   The approach is conceptually simple, combining existing [[passage retrieval]] techniques with a [[sequence-to-sequence model]], yet yields substantial performance gains.

# Limitations

The paper does not explicitly list limitations. However, potential limitations include the reliance on the quality of the initial [[passage retrieval]] step; if relevant passages are not retrieved, the generative model cannot produce a correct answer. While the [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] architecture improves scalability, the computational cost still increases linearly with the number of retrieved passages, which could be a factor for very high-latency applications or extremely large numbers of passages. The generative model's ability to synthesize information is still bounded by its own capacity and potential for hallucination, even with strong evidence.

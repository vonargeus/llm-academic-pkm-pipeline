# Summary

This paper introduces [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (FiD), a simple yet highly effective approach for [[Open Domain Question Answering]] that combines [[passage retrieval]] with [[generative models]]. Instead of relying solely on the parametric memory of massive language models, the authors retrieve relevant text passages from [[Wikipedia]] and feed them to a [[sequence-to-sequence]] model to generate the final answer. The key finding is that processing retrieved passages independently in the encoder and performing attention over their concatenated representations in the decoder scales efficiently to a large number of passages (up to 100). This structural design allows the model to effectively aggregate evidence from multiple sources, achieving state-of-the-art results on several open-domain benchmarks.

# Main Contribution

- Proposed the [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (FiD) architecture, which decouples passage encoding from joint decoding to enable efficient evidence aggregation.
- Demonstrated that [[generative models]] scale monotonically and efficiently in performance when retrieving up to 100 passages, unlike traditional [[extractive models]].
- Achieved state-of-the-art results on the [[Natural Questions]], [[TriviaQA]], and [[SQuAD Open]] benchmarks using standard pretrained [[sequence-to-sequence]] models like [[T5]].

# Research Problem

While large-scale [[generative models]] can store factual knowledge within their parameters, relying entirely on parametric memory requires models with billions of parameters, which are computationally expensive to train and query. Conversely, traditional retrieval-based [[extractive models]] struggle to aggregate and synthesize evidence when answers are distributed across multiple passages. This paper addresses the gap of how to design a retrieval-augmented generative framework that can scale to a large number of retrieved passages and efficiently combine evidence from multiple sources without incurring quadratic computational costs in the encoder.

# Method

The [[Fusion-in-Decoder]] (FiD) method consists of a two-step pipeline: retrieval and reading. For the retrieval step, the system retrieves supporting passages from [[Wikipedia]] using either [[sparse representations]] (via [[BM25]]) or [[dense representations]] (via [[DPR]]). 

For the reading step, the system employs a pretrained [[encoder-decoder]] model (such as [[T5]] or [[BART]]). The core architectural innovation lies in how the retrieved passages are processed:
1. **Independent Encoding**: Each retrieved passage, along with its title and the original question, is formatted with special tokens (e.g., `question:`, `title:`, `context:`) and processed *independently* by the encoder. Because self-attention is performed only within individual passages, the computational complexity of the encoding step scales linearly ($O(N)$) rather than quadratically ($O(N^2)$) with the number of passages $N$.
2. **Joint Decoding**: The decoder performs attention over the concatenated representations of all retrieved passages. This allows the model to perform evidence fusion across all retrieved contexts simultaneously when generating the final answer tokens.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The authors evaluated their approach on three open-domain question answering datasets:
- [[Natural Questions]]: Google search queries where answers are restricted to 5 tokens or fewer.
- [[TriviaQA]]: An unfiltered trivia dataset used for open-domain QA.
- [[SQuAD Open]]: The open-domain variant of the SQuAD v1.1 reading comprehension dataset.

The knowledge source used was [[Wikipedia]] (dumps from Dec. 2018 for NQ/TriviaQA, and Dec. 2016 for SQuAD). Retrieval was performed using [[BM25]] and [[DPR]] to fetch up to 100 passages. The reader models were initialized with [[T5]] (base and large configurations).

# Evaluation Metrics

- [[EM]] (Exact Match): Used to measure the percentage of predicted answers that match the gold standard answers exactly, which is the standard metric for open-domain QA.
- [[F1]] score: Used to evaluate the token-level overlap between the predicted and ground-truth answers, providing a more flexible measure of generation quality.

# Key Results

- **State-of-the-Art Performance**: FiD (large) achieved outstanding results, outperforming existing extractive and generative baselines:
  - **[[Natural Questions]]**: **51.4%** [[EM]] (compared to 44.5% for RAG and 41.5% for DPR).
  - **[[TriviaQA]]**: **67.6%** [[EM]] on the open-domain test set and **80.1%** [[EM]] on the hidden test set.
  - **[[SQuAD Open]]**: **56.7%** [[EM]] and **63.2%** [[F1]].
- **Scaling with Passages**: The performance of FiD improved monotonically as the number of retrieved passages increased from 5 to 100. For example, on [[Natural Questions]], FiD (base) improved from ~40% [[EM]] with 5 passages to over **48%** [[EM]] with 100 passages.
- **Robustness to Retrieval Quality**: The generative reader proved highly robust, successfully extracting correct answers even when the retriever returned noisy or weakly related passages.

# Limitations

The paper does not deeply explore the joint training of the retriever and the reader, relying instead on frozen retrievers ([[BM25]] or [[DPR]]). Additionally, while independent encoding scales linearly, processing 100 passages still incurs a non-trivial computational overhead during inference, particularly in the cross-attention layers of the decoder.

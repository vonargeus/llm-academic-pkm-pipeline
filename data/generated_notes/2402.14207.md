# Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of using [[Large Language Models]] ([[LLMs]]) to generate [[grounded writing]], [[long-form articles]] similar to [[Wikipedia pages]], from scratch, particularly focusing on the crucial [[pre-writing stage]]. They propose [[STORM]], a system that researches topics and prepares outlines by discovering diverse [[perspectives]], simulating [[multi-turn conversations]] with a [[topic expert]] grounded on [[Internet sources]], and curating the acquired information. Evaluating with [[FreshWiki]] and expert [[Wikipedia]] editors, [[STORM]] significantly improves the [[organization]] (25% absolute increase) and [[breadth]] (10% increase) of generated articles compared to an [[outline]]-driven [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation]] ([[RAG]]) baseline.

# Research Problem

This paper tackles the underexplored problem of enabling [[Large Language Models]] ([[LLMs]]) to write [[grounded writing]] and well-organized [[long-form articles]] from scratch, akin to [[Wikipedia pages]]. While [[LLMs]] show impressive writing capabilities, applying them to complex [[expository writing]] that requires thorough research and planning in the [[pre-writing stage]] remains a significant challenge. Prior work on [[Wikipedia]] article generation often bypasses this stage by assuming outlines or reference documents are already provided, neglecting the demanding [[information acquisition]] and [[outline generation]] tasks that require advanced [[information literacy skills]]. This gap highlights the need for systems that can automate the initial research and planning phases to facilitate in-depth learning and reduce expert effort.

# Method

The authors propose [[STORM]] (Synthesis of Topic Outlines through Retrieval and Multi-perspective Question Asking), a novel system designed to automate the [[pre-writing stage]] for generating [[Wikipedia-like articles]]. [[STORM]] operates on two core hypotheses: (1) diverse [[perspectives]] lead to varied questions, and (2) formulating in-depth questions requires [[iterative research]].

[[STORM]] employs a multi-stage approach. First, it discovers diverse [[perspectives]] relevant to a given topic by retrieving and analyzing existing [[Wikipedia articles]] on similar subjects. This allows the [[LLM]] to personify different viewpoints for subsequent question asking, moving beyond the surface-level questions typically generated by [[Direct Prompting]]. Second, to facilitate [[iterative research]] and elicit in-depth questions, [[STORM]] simulates [[multi-turn conversations]]. In this phase, [[LLMs]], embodying specific [[perspectives]] (termed [[Perspective-Guided Question Asking]]), pose questions to a simulated [[topic expert]]. The answers provided by this expert are strictly [[grounded writing]] on trusted [[Internet sources]], preventing [[hallucinations]] and leveraging [[external sources]] rather than just [[parametric knowledge]]. This conversational approach is referred to as [[Conversational Question Asking]]. Finally, based on the [[LLM]]'s internal knowledge and the comprehensive information collected through these simulated conversations, [[STORM]] synthesizes and creates a structured [[outline]] that can then serve as a foundation for developing the full-length article. This method differs from prior [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation]] ([[RAG]]) approaches by actively researching and structuring information in a human-like pre-writing process, rather than simply retrieving documents based on a given query.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

To evaluate the system's capacity for generating [[long-form articles]] from scratch, the authors curated [[FreshWiki]], a specialized dataset of recent, high-quality [[Wikipedia articles]]. This dataset was specifically designed to mitigate the issue of [[data leakage]], where [[LLMs]] might have been trained on existing [[Wikipedia]] content, by focusing on articles created or heavily edited after the training cutoff of the tested [[LLMs]].

The selection process for [[FreshWiki]] involved identifying the top 100 most-edited pages for each month from February 2022 to September 2023. These articles were then filtered to include only those assessed as B-class quality or above by ORES, ensuring high quality. List articles and those without subsections were excluded to focus on structured [[expository writing]]. The task was decomposed into two parts: first, conducting research to generate an [[outline]] and collect a set of reference documents; second, using this [[outline]] and references to produce the full-length article. The experiments compared [[STORM]] against an [[outline]]-driven [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation]] ([[RAG]]) baseline.

# Evaluation Metrics

The evaluation of [[STORM]] focused on assessing the quality of the generated [[outline]] and the final article, particularly emphasizing the [[pre-writing stage]].

-   **[[outline assessments]]**: Metrics specifically formulated to evaluate the quality of the generated [[outline]] against human-written articles, indicating a comprehensive understanding of the topic.
-   **[[organization]]**: A qualitative metric assessing how well the generated articles are structured and logically presented.
-   **[[breadth]]**: A qualitative metric evaluating the comprehensiveness and coverage of the topic within the generated articles.
-   **Expert Feedback**: Qualitative assessments gathered from experienced [[Wikipedia]] editors, providing insights into the practical utility and challenges of the generated content.

# Key Results

-   [[STORM]] significantly outperformed an [[outline]]-driven [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation]] ([[RAG]]) baseline in human evaluations.
-   Articles generated by [[STORM]] were deemed more [[organized]] by a 25% absolute increase compared to the baseline.
-   [[STORM]]'s articles showed a 10% increase in [[breadth]] of coverage compared to the baseline.
-   Expert feedback from experienced [[Wikipedia]] editors confirmed the effectiveness of [[STORM]] in generating [[grounded writing]] and well-structured [[long-form articles]].
-   The expert evaluation also highlighted new challenges for future research, specifically [[source bias transfer]] from [[Internet sources]] and the [[over-association of unrelated facts]] by [[LLMs]].

# Limitations

The expert feedback identified key challenges for future research, including the potential for [[source bias transfer]] from [[Internet sources]] into the generated articles. Another limitation noted was the tendency of [[LLMs]] to fabricate connections, leading to the [[over-association of unrelated facts]] within the generated content.

# Summary

The paper introduces [[BayesVLM]], a post-hoc method designed to provide [[Uncertainty Quantification]] for pre-trained [[Vision-Language Models]] (VLMs) like [[CLIP]] and [[SigLIP]]. By applying a [[Laplace approximation]] to the weights of the final projection layers, the authors transform deterministic models into probabilistic ones without requiring expensive retraining or architectural changes. The main finding is that [[BayesVLM]] significantly improves model [[Calibration]] and enables more sample-efficient [[Active Learning]] by effectively capturing [[epistemic uncertainty]] in the [[joint latent space]].

# Main Contribution

- Proposed [[BayesVLM]], an efficient and effective post-hoc method for [[Uncertainty Quantification]] in pre-trained [[Vision-Language Models]] that requires no additional training.
- Derived a novel analytical expression for the distribution over [[cosine similarity]], allowing for efficient [[uncertainty propagation]] from the weights to the model output.
- Demonstrated that the Bayesian formulation reduces overconfident misclassifications and improves performance in [[Active Learning]] and [[zero-shot classification]] tasks.

# Research Problem

Deterministic [[Vision-Language Models]] (VLMs) map images and text to a [[joint latent space]] but fail to account for the uncertainty inherent in these mappings, especially when faced with [[domain shift]] in downstream tasks. This lack of [[Uncertainty Quantification]] leads to overconfident and potentially dangerous predictions in safety-critical applications. Existing solutions often involve training probabilistic models from scratch or using expensive adapters, creating a need for a training-free, post-hoc approach that maintains the efficiency of the original VLM.

# Method

The authors propose [[BayesVLM]], which utilizes a [[Laplace approximation]] to estimate a [[Bayesian posterior]] over the parameters of the last layers (the projection heads) of a VLM. This approach treats the image and text encoders as independent probabilistic models, adhering to an i.i.d. assumption for efficient [[posterior inference]]. 

A key technical contribution is the derivation of an analytical expression for the distribution of the [[cosine similarity]] between the probabilistic image and text embeddings. This allows the model to propagate uncertainty through the similarity calculation without resorting to computationally expensive Monte Carlo sampling. The method is model-agnostic and can be applied to any VLM architecture that uses a [[joint latent space]] and [[cosine similarity]] for assessment.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The method was evaluated primarily using the [[EuroSAT]] dataset, which contains Sentinel-2 satellite imagery for land use and land cover classification. The experiments utilized pre-trained [[OpenCLIP]] (ViT-H-14) and [[SigLIP]] models. The setup included [[zero-shot classification]] benchmarks to test [[Calibration]] and an [[Active Learning]] framework where the model's uncertainty estimates were used to select the most informative samples for fine-tuning.

# Evaluation Metrics

- [[Entropy]]: Used to quantify the predictive uncertainty and identify instances where the model is overconfident.
- [[Error rate]]: Measured to evaluate the accuracy of the model in classification tasks.
- [[Calibration]]: Assessed to determine how well the predicted probabilities align with actual empirical frequencies.
- [[Sample efficiency]]: Evaluated in the context of [[Active Learning]] to see how much data is required to reach a certain performance threshold.

# Key Results

- [[BayesVLM]] showed a substantial reduction in overconfident predictions compared to deterministic baselines; for instance, on [[EuroSAT]], the Bayesian model exhibited much better alignment between [[Entropy]] and [[Error rate]].
- In [[Active Learning]] scenarios, using [[BayesVLM]] for sample selection led to a significantly lower misclassification rate after adaptation compared to standard fine-tuning of [[OpenCLIP]].
- The analytical [[uncertainty propagation]] was found to be highly efficient, introducing little to no computational overhead during the inference phase.
- The method proved robust even when applied to VLMs pre-trained on proprietary or large-scale datasets like [[LAION]].

# Limitations

The authors note that while the method is post-hoc and efficient, it primarily focuses on the uncertainty in the final projection layers, which may not capture all sources of uncertainty present in the deep backbone of the encoders. Additionally, the [[Laplace approximation]] assumes a Gaussian shape for the posterior, which might be a simplification of the true weight distribution in complex models.

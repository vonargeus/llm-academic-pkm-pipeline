# Summary

This paper addresses the limitation of existing [[Retrieval-Augmented Generation]] (RAG) models that retrieve only short, contiguous text chunks, hindering a holistic understanding of document context. The authors introduce [[RAPTOR]], a novel method that recursively embeds, clusters, and summarizes text chunks to construct a multi-layered [[tree structure]]. This approach enables retrieval at different levels of abstraction, allowing [[Large Language Models]] (LLMs) to integrate information across lengthy documents more effectively. [[RAPTOR]] demonstrates significant improvements over traditional RAG methods, achieving new [[state-of-the-art]] results on complex [[Question Answering]] benchmarks like [[NarrativeQA]], [[QASPER]], and [[QuALITY]].

# Main Contribution

- Introduces [[RAPTOR]], a novel approach for recursively embedding, clustering, and summarizing text chunks to construct a [[tree structure]] with differing levels of summarization.
- Enables retrieval from this [[tree structure]], integrating information across lengthy documents at various levels of abstraction to provide richer context to [[Large Language Models]].
- Demonstrates significant improvements over traditional [[retrieval-augmented language models]] through controlled experiments on several tasks.
- Achieves new [[state-of-the-art]] results on complex [[Question Answering]] tasks, including [[NarrativeQA]], [[QASPER]], and [[QuALITY]] benchmarks, particularly when coupled with [[GPT-4]].

# Research Problem

The core research problem is that existing [[retrieval-augmented language models]] (RALMs) predominantly retrieve only short, contiguous text chunks from a corpus. This limitation restricts their ability to gain a holistic understanding of the overall document context and leverage large-scale discourse structure. Consequently, these models struggle with tasks requiring the integration of knowledge from multiple, potentially distant, parts of a text, such as understanding an entire book for complex [[Question Answering]]. While [[Large Language Models]] (LLMs) can store [[parametric knowledge]], updating it is difficult, making [[retrieval augmentation]] crucial for current and domain-specific knowledge, but its current form is insufficient for deep contextual understanding.

# Method

[[RAPTOR]] (Recursive Abstractive Processing for Tree-Organized Retrieval) is designed to overcome the limitations of short-chunk retrieval by building a recursive [[tree structure]] that balances broad thematic comprehension with granular details. Unlike prior methods that rely on text adjacency for grouping, [[RAPTOR]] groups nodes based on [[semantic similarity]], allowing it to identify and connect distant interdependencies within a text.

The construction of the [[RAPTOR]] tree begins by segmenting the retrieval corpus into short, contiguous text chunks, typically around 100 tokens, while preserving sentence boundaries to maintain contextual coherence. These initial chunks are then embedded using a [[BERT]]-based encoder like [[SBERT]] (specifically, multi-qa-mpnet-base-cos-v1), forming the [[leaf nodes]] of the tree.

Next, a [[clustering algorithm]] is applied to group these [[vector embeddings]] of text chunks based on their similarity. Once clusters are formed, a [[Large Language Model]] (LLM) is employed to [[summarize]] the grouped texts. These generated summaries are then re-embedded, and the entire cycle of embedding, clustering, and summarization is repeated. This recursive process continues, building the tree from the bottom up, until further meaningful clustering becomes infeasible, resulting in a multi-layered, hierarchical representation of the original documents. The system is designed for computational efficiency, scaling linearly in both build time and token expenditure.

For querying the constructed tree, [[RAPTOR]] offers two distinct strategies: [[tree traversal]] and [[collapsed tree]]. The [[tree traversal]] method navigates the tree layer-by-layer, pruning and selecting the most relevant nodes at each level. In contrast, the [[collapsed tree]] method evaluates nodes collectively across all layers to identify the most pertinent information for a given query.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The authors evaluated [[RAPTOR]] on three distinct [[Question Answering]] datasets, each presenting different challenges related to document length and complexity:
-   [[NarrativeQA]]: This dataset involves free-text response questions based on entire books and movie scripts, requiring deep understanding and integration of information across long documents.
-   [[QASPER]]: Focused on full-text NLP papers, this dataset tests the ability to extract and synthesize information from scientific literature.
-   [[QuALITY]]: This benchmark consists of multiple-choice questions derived from medium-length passages, assessing comprehension and reasoning over substantial text blocks.

Experiments were set up to compare [[RAPTOR]]'s performance against traditional [[retrieval augmentation]] techniques. The system was coupled with various [[Large Language Models]] for generating answers, including [[UnifiedQA]], [[GPT-3]], and [[GPT-4]], to assess its effectiveness across different reader models. Initial preprocessing involved segmenting documents into approximately 100-token chunks, ensuring sentence integrity.

# Evaluation Metrics

The primary metric used to evaluate the performance of [[RAPTOR]] across the various [[Question Answering]] tasks was [[absolute accuracy]].

# Key Results

-   [[RAPTOR]] consistently demonstrated significant improvements over traditional [[retrieval augmentation]] methods across all evaluated tasks.
-   When coupled with [[GPT-4]], [[RAPTOR]] achieved new [[state-of-the-art]] results on the complex [[Question Answering]] benchmarks: [[NarrativeQA]], [[QASPER]], and [[QuALITY]].
-   Specifically, [[RAPTOR]] combined with [[GPT-4]] improved the best performance on the [[QuALITY]] benchmark by 20% in [[absolute accuracy]].
-   Even when paired with [[UnifiedQA]], a less powerful [[Large Language Model]] than [[GPT-4]], [[RAPTOR]] was able to achieve [[state-of-the-art]] results on some of the evaluated tasks, highlighting the robustness of its retrieval mechanism.

# Limitations

While [[RAPTOR]] offers significant advancements, the inherent nature of [[text summarization]] can sometimes lead to a loss of granular details, even though the method aims to balance abstraction with detail. The computational cost, particularly for the [[Large Language Model]]-based summarization step, could still be substantial for extremely vast corpora, despite the system's linear scaling properties.

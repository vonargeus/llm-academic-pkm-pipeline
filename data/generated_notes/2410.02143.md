# Summary

This paper introduces a [[Plug-and-play framework]] for [[controllable generation]] in [[discrete masked models]] (such as [[BERT]], [[MaskGIT]], and [[ESM]]). The authors solve the problem of sampling from a posterior distribution or satisfying specific constraints without requiring task-specific fine-tuning or gradient information. They achieve this by combining [[mean-field approximation]], [[importance sampling]], and an [[iterative unmasking and remasking]] strategy to filter samples based on a [[reward function]]. The main finding is that high-quality, controlled discrete sequences can be generated efficiently with a small number of model queries and [[Monte Carlo estimation]] samples.

# Main Contribution

- Introduced the first [[Plug-and-play framework|plug-and-play]] controllable sampler for [[discrete masked models]] that requires no fine-tuning.
- Developed a framework based on [[mean-field approximation]] and [[iterative unmasking and remasking]] to handle non-differentiable [[reward function]]s.
- Demonstrated a versatile approach applicable to diverse domains, including [[Bayesian inverse problems]] and [[protein design]].

# Research Problem

The paper addresses the challenge of [[controllable generation]] in [[discrete masked models]]. While continuous diffusion models have established methods for guided generation, discrete models typically rely on resource-intensive fine-tuning or require differentiable control criteria. This gap limits the utility of pre-trained discrete models for tasks like [[posterior sampling]], [[constrained generation]], and [[Bayesian inference]] where the control signal (reward) might be non-differentiable or provided by an external black-box evaluator.

# Method

The proposed method is a fine-tuning-free [[Plug-and-play framework]] that operates through [[iterative unmasking and remasking]]. At each inference step, the algorithm uses the [[masked model]] to obtain conditional probabilities for all masked positions. It then applies a [[mean-field approximation]] to treat these positions as independent discrete variables, allowing for efficient [[Monte Carlo estimation]]. 

Specifically, the algorithm draws $K$ candidate samples from the approximated distribution and weights them using [[importance sampling]] based on a target [[reward function]]. The best-performing sample is selected, and a portion of the newly unmasked tokens is remasked according to an [[unmasking schedule]] (retaining only the most confident predictions). This process repeats until the sequence is fully unmasked. Unlike prior work, this approach does not require a [[conditional score]] or gradient-based optimization, making it compatible with any discrete [[reward function]].

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The authors evaluated their framework on two primary tasks:
1.  **[[Integer sequences]]**: Generating sequences of integers that satisfy specific equality constraints (e.g., the sum of elements must equal a target value).
2.  **[[Protein sequences]]**: Using the [[ESM]] (Evolutionary Scale Modeling) series to design protein sequences with specific biological properties.
The experiments compared the proposed sampler against standard [[iterative decoding]] and analyzed the impact of the number of [[Monte Carlo estimation]] samples ($K$) and the number of queries to the [[masked model]].

# Evaluation Metrics

- **[[Number of queries to the masked model]]**: Used to measure the computational efficiency and inference speed of the generation process.
- **[[Monte Carlo sample count]]**: Used to evaluate the trade-off between the quality of the [[importance sampling]] and the computational overhead of evaluating the [[reward function]].

# Key Results

- The framework successfully generates samples that satisfy complex constraints with as few as 10 queries to the [[masked model]].
- Using approximately 1000 [[Monte Carlo estimation]] samples ($K=1000$) provides a robust balance between sampling accuracy and speed.
- In [[protein design]] tasks, the method effectively optimizes sequences toward desired biological rewards without any task-specific training.
- The approach proved effective for [[Bayesian inverse problems]], demonstrating its ability to sample from complex posterior distributions in discrete spaces.

# Limitations

The method relies on a [[mean-field approximation]], which assumes independence between masked positions at each step; this may fail to capture complex local dependencies if the unmasking steps are too large. Additionally, while it reduces model queries, the [[importance sampling]] step requires $K$ evaluations of the [[reward function]], which could become a bottleneck if the reward function itself is computationally expensive.

# Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of improving the reasoning capabilities of [[small language models]] (SLMs) by leveraging explanations generated by [[large language models]] (LLMs). The authors systematically explore three explanation generation methods—[[chain of thought prompting]], [[rationalization prompting]], and a novel [[hybrid prompting]] approach—to generate high-quality training explanations. They then train the SLMs using a [[multi-task learning]] framework, introducing a new method called [[MT-CoT (Multi-Task Chain of Thought)]] where the model learns to derive answers directly from generated explanations. The main finding is that distilling LLM-generated explanations consistently and significantly improves SLM performance, even outperforming a 60x larger [[GPT-3]] model on [[CommonsenseQA]].

# Main Contribution

- Showed that [[multi-task learning]] with explanations from LLMs consistently and significantly improves strong [[T5]] single-task [[fine-tuning]] baselines.
- Proposed a [[hybrid prompting]] approach to generate high-quality explanations from LLMs by combining [[chain of thought prompting]] and [[rationalization prompting]].
- Proposed [[MT-CoT (Multi-Task Chain of Thought)]] to allow [[small language models]] to learn to derive answers from explanations.
- Demonstrated that the proposed method can outperform a 60x larger [[GPT-3]] model (175B) on [[CommonsenseQA]] by up to 9.5% in accuracy.

# Research Problem

The research problem is how to leverage explanations generated by [[large language models]] to improve the training and reasoning capabilities of [[small language models]], which are highly desirable for real-world deployment due to their low computational and storage costs. While LLMs excel at reasoning when prompted with explanations, transferring this capability to smaller models remains a challenge. Previous attempts using human-annotated explanations failed to show significant improvements, likely due to the noisy, inconsistent, and grammatically incorrect nature of crowdsourced data. This paper addresses this gap by utilizing LLMs to automatically generate high-quality, consistent explanations to act as a distillation source for training smaller reasoners.

# Method

The authors propose a framework that first generates explanations from an LLM and then uses them to train a [[small language model]] (specifically [[T5]]) via [[multi-task learning]]. They explore three prompting strategies for explanation generation: (1) [[chain of thought prompting]] (CoT), which generates explanations before the prediction but rejects them if the prediction is incorrect; (2) [[rationalization prompting]], which generates explanations conditioned on the golden label; and (3) a novel [[hybrid prompting]] approach. The hybrid approach uses CoT-generated explanations when the LLM's prediction is correct, and falls back to rationalization prompting when the prediction is incorrect, ensuring a complete and high-quality explanation set.

To train the SLM, the authors utilize a multi-task learning framework to avoid the performance degradation associated with simple sequence concatenation. They evaluate standard multi-task setups like MT-Re (predicting explanations given the input) and MT-Ra (predicting the label given the input and explanation). Crucially, they introduce [[MT-CoT (Multi-Task Chain of Thought)]], a multi-task setup where the SLM is trained on two tasks: directly predicting the answer, and generating the explanation followed by the answer. This design encourages the small model to learn the causal reasoning path of deriving the final answer from its own generated explanation.

# Dataset / Experimental Setup

The primary dataset used for evaluation is [[CommonsenseQA]], a dataset designed to test complex commonsense reasoning. The authors use a large language model (such as [[GPT-3]] 175B) to generate explanations for the training set using few-shot prompts containing human-written explanation exemplars. These generated explanations are then used to train smaller [[T5]] models (e.g., T5-base, T5-large, T5-3B) under different multi-task learning configurations.

# Evaluation Metrics

- **[[accuracy]]**: Used to measure the classification performance of the small language models on the reasoning tasks.
- **Human Evaluation**: Used to qualitatively assess the logical consistency, grammatical correctness, and overall quality of the explanations generated by the trained small models.

# Key Results

- Multi-task learning with LLM-generated explanations consistently and significantly outperformed standard single-task [[fine-tuning]] baselines across all model sizes.
- The proposed [[MT-CoT (Multi-Task Chain of Thought)]] combined with [[hybrid prompting]] achieved the best performance, outperforming standard fine-tuning by up to 8.1% in accuracy.
- The trained [[T5]]-3B model using the proposed method outperformed a 60x larger [[GPT-3]] (175B) model by up to 9.5% in accuracy on [[CommonsenseQA]].
- Human evaluation confirmed that the small models trained with this method generate high-quality, logically sound explanations to justify their predictions, advancing [[explainable AI]].

# Limitations

The method relies heavily on the quality of explanations generated by the teacher LLM, meaning any systematic biases or hallucinations in the LLM's reasoning could be transferred to the smaller model. Additionally, the framework was primarily evaluated on commonsense reasoning tasks like [[CommonsenseQA]], and its generalizability to highly specialized or technical domains remains to be fully explored.
